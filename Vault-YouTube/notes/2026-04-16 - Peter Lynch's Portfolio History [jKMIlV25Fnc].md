## Summary
- Peter Lynch ran the Magellan Fund for 13 years, achieving 29% CAGR (604% total vs S&P 500's 223%).
- His portfolio had massive drawdowns every year—often larger than the S&P—because contrarian investing requires buying on downtrends.
- Key lesson: outperformance comes with larger short-term pain, not smoother returns.

## Key Points
- **Track record**: 29% annual CAGR over 13 years; 604% total return vs S&P 223%.
- **Not a straight line**: Major drawdowns of 56%, 27%, 42%, 32% during his tenure—all worse than the S&P's own drops.
- **Why worse drawdowns**: Contrarian investors buy undervalued stocks on downtrends. Cheap stocks can get cheaper. This is the price of buying at the right level.
- **The contrarian process**: Buy what no one else dares to buy (to get in when undervalued). Cheap stocks on downtrends will temporarily fall further before recovering.
- **The long-term payoff**: Accepting worse short-term volatility sets up superior long-term performance.
- **Implication**: If you can't stomach 30%+ drawdowns, you can't capture Lynch-level returns.
