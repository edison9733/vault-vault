## Summary
- Hedging (buying put options, shorting, inverse ETFs) sounds logical as portfolio insurance but in practice reduces long-term returns.
- The author stopped hedging because 4 out of 5 times the market reverses UP quickly after he hedged, costing him money on the hedge while missing the recovery.
- The asymmetry is unfavorable: occasional hedging wins don't offset the repeated losses from missed recoveries.

## Key Points
- **What hedging means**: Buying put options, shorting the market, or purchasing inverse ETFs to profit when the market falls.
- **The problem with hedging**: Only 1 in 5 times does the market fall significantly enough and stay down long enough to make your hedge profitable.
- **4 out of 5 times**: The market reverses back up quickly (especially post-2020 with Fed support), and your put options expire worthless or your shorts get squeezed.
- **Net long-term effect**: Hedging reduces your overall compound return because the losses on failed hedges outweigh the occasional wins.
- **The alternative**: Simply hold great companies through the volatility; rebalance, don't hedge.
