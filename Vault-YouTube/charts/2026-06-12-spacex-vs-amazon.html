Step 1 — What the Words Mean

Market cap — the total price of a company.
Market cap = share price × number of shares. "SpaceX at $1.75 trillion" means buying every single share would cost $1.75 trillion.
Revenue (Sales) — all the money customers paid the company in one year, before any costs.
Amazon: $716 billion/year. SpaceX: $16 billion/year.
EBITDA — Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation and Amortisation.
A measure of "how much profit does the core business actually make?" Ignore the accounting noise, just: did you earn money?
P/S ratio (Price-to-Sales) — market cap ÷ yearly revenue.
P/S = 3 means: "for every $1 of annual sales, investors pay $3." P/S = 109 means: "for every $1 of annual sales, investors pay $109." Higher P/S = more expensive.
P/EBITDA ratio — market cap ÷ yearly EBITDA.
Same idea but using profit instead of sales. P/EBITDA = 218 means you pay $218 for every $1 of profit the company makes per year.

Step 4 — What Adam Khoo Concluded

At roughly the same total price (~$2 trillion), SpaceX has 45× less revenue and 18× less profit than Amazon.

To justify a P/S of 109×, SpaceX would need to grow its revenue 36× — just to match the price Amazon trades at today, which is already considered a reasonable price.

Adam Khoo is not saying SpaceX is a bad company. He is saying: the IPO price leaves almost no room for error. If SpaceX grows slower than expected, the stock will fall hard.


His alternative suggestion: Instead of buying SpaceX at 109× sales, buy Alphabet — which already owns 7% of SpaceX. That stake is worth roughly $6–10 billion and is not included in Alphabet's stock price at all.

You get SpaceX exposure for free, on top of Google's search, cloud, and advertising business trading at a much more reasonable valuation.
